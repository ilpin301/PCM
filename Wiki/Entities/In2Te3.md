# In₂Te₃

In₂Te₃ is a **covalently bonded** compound that is **not** a phase-change material. It is used as the covalent layer in metavalent–covalent PCM superlattices, where it acts as a stabilizing, confining counterpart to a metavalent PCM such as GST-124.

## Description

Unlike the metavalent chalcogenide PCMs, In₂Te₃ does not undergo reliable, reversible amorphization. In Widmann's GST-124/In₂Te₃ superlattices it provides a fundamentally different interface than the metavalent/metavalent systems studied previously (see [[pcm-superlattices]]). It is also of interest for photovoltaics and electronic devices in its own right.

## Key Attributes

| Property | Value |
|----------|-------|
| Crystal structure | Zinc-blende type, space group F-43m |
| Bonding | Covalent (contrast with metavalent PCMs) |
| Vacancies | ~1/3 of indium lattice sites intrinsically vacant (vacancy-rich) |
| Phase-change behavior | None (does not amorphize reliably) |
| Optical absorption (660 nm) | Lower than Sb₂Te₃ at the same wavelength |

The intrinsic vacancies compensate the valence-electron imbalance and stabilize the structure, strongly influencing its properties.

## Behavior under laser pulses

- Strong laser pulses **reduce the reflectivity** of an In₂Te₃ reference film, but **EBSD excludes amorphization** — the change is some other structural modification, not a true phase change.
- The reflectivity transition is **abrupt/sharp** (vs. the smooth, gradual transition of conventional GST), reflecting its different structural response.

## In superlattices and on annealing

- In GST-124/In₂Te₃ superlattices, thinner In₂Te₃ layers give thinner supercells, more interfaces, and more efficient switching.
- **Annealing ≥300 °C** drives intermixing: a new XRD peak near 13° is attributed to **InSb** formation (In₂Te₃ reacting with the Sb₂Te₃ seed layer), which degrades interface quality and switching efficiency.
- Formation energy (Materials Project): In₂Te₃ −0.405 eV/atom — more stable than InSb (−0.238), Sb₂Te₃ (−0.383), and GST-124 (−0.353).
